Challenges and enabling factors in Côte d’Ivoire compared with Ghana

I. Background to the Study

Influencer marketing is when brands use social media personalities, known as influencers, to promote their products or services. These influencers often have many followers who trust their opinions. Currently, influencer marketing is growing rapidly worldwide. Many people trust influencers more than traditional ads like TV or radio.

In Africa, this marketing method is also expanding quickly. Many young people use smartphones and social media daily. Countries like Ghana have already built a strong influencer marketing system. In Ghana, many influencers regularly work with brands and get paid for promoting products. Agencies and events also help connect influencers with companies, making influencer marketing in Ghana more organized and professional.

However, the situation differs in Côte d’Ivoire. Although the country has many well-known influencers with large followings, influencer marketing remains underdeveloped. Many brands still prefer traditional advertising instead of collaborating with influencers. Furthermore, social media users in Côte d’Ivoire primarily use platforms for entertainment and gossip, sometimes known as “gbairai,” rather than discovering products or services. This makes it harder for influencers to persuade their audience to make purchases.

Another issue is the lack of structure in the industry. There are no clear rules, pricing standards, or strong agencies to manage influencer-brand relationships. Additionally, some global platforms do not fully support monetization in Côte d’Ivoire, limiting income opportunities for influencers.

At the same time, digital consumption habits are changing across West Africa. Mobile internet access, short-form video, and in-app shopping features are shaping how consumers search for information, compare alternatives, and form opinions about brands. As a result, influencer marketing is no longer limited to celebrity endorsement; it increasingly includes micro- and nano-influencers who serve smaller but more engaged communities. For small and medium-sized enterprises, influencers can function as a low-cost channel for awareness, trial, and reputation building, especially when traditional media budgets are high.

In Côte d’Ivoire, this potential is not consistently converted into structured campaigns. Many collaborations remain informal and are often negotiated without clear deliverables, timelines, or disclosure requirements. Brands may hesitate because they cannot easily connect influencer activity to outcomes such as leads, store visits, or sales, while creators may struggle with payment systems, contract enforcement, and limited access to platform monetization. These practical constraints affect how professional the market becomes and whether influencer marketing is viewed as a credible investment rather than a risky experiment.

Because of these differences, it is important to study the challenges of influencer marketing in Côte d’Ivoire and compare them with Ghana. This will provide insights into what is missing and how the system can improve.

II. Problem Statement

Despite being one of the largest economies in West Africa, Côte d’Ivoire's influencer marketing is not as developed as Ghana's. Many brands in Côte d’Ivoire still rely on traditional advertising, such as TV and radio, rather than using influencers.

The gap between the two countries is clear and noticeable. In Ghana, creators like @giselaamponsah rely almost entirely on paid brand partnerships, integrating sponsored content so well that audiences cannot tell ads from regular posts (Nkonkonsa, 2025). Liu and Zheng (2024) identify this practice as essential for building effective parasocial relationships between influencers and consumers—a dynamic that boosts brand credibility and purchase intent. Ghana also has active influencer management firms in Accra and platforms like Wowzi, which have allowed brands to work with 7,432 influencers across 18 brands in just six months (Wowzi, 2024). No similar infrastructure is present in Côte d'Ivoire. Monetization is another issue. Influencers in Côte d’Ivoire cannot easily earn money directly from platforms like YouTube or TikTok, so they mostly rely on brand deals, which are quite uncommon. There’s also a cultural aspect; many social media users in Côte d’Ivoire prefer platforms for entertainment and gossip, reducing the effectiveness of influencer marketing in shifting consumer behavior. Additionally, trust between brands and influencers is low. Many companies are unsure if influencer marketing will yield positive results. They also lack knowledge about measuring success (ROI). Lastly, there is limited academic research on influencer marketing in Côte d’Ivoire, with most studies focusing on countries like Ghana, Nigeria, or South Africa, creating a knowledge gap.

Given these problems, it is crucial to study the barriers to influencer marketing in Côte d’Ivoire and compare them with those in Ghana to identify solutions.

III. Research Objectives

Main Objective

To investigate the challenges of effective influencer marketing and brand collaboration in Côte d'Ivoire and compare these with the factors supporting Ghana's more developed influencer ecosystem, in order to provide actionable recommendations for improving digital marketing in Côte d’Ivoire.

Specific Objectives

  1. To identify the main barriers affecting influencer marketing in Côte d’Ivoire.
  2. To examine the factors that contribute to successful influencer marketing in Ghana.
  3. To compare influencer practices between Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana and suggest improvements.

IV. Research Questions

  1. What are the main barriers to influencer marketing in Côte d’Ivoire?
  2. What factors contribute to successful influencer marketing in Ghana?
  3. What lessons can Côte d’Ivoire learn from Ghana?

V. Literature Review

1. Concept and Global Growth of Influencer Marketing

Influencer marketing involves using individuals with established social media followings to promote brands or products to specific audiences. Lou and Yuan (2019) created a foundational model showing that the informational and entertainment value of influencer content positively influences consumer trust in branded posts and their subsequent purchase intentions. Their research established that message value and credibility are the key elements of effective influencer marketing. The global influencer marketing sector was valued at USD 16.98 billion in 2023 and is expected to reach USD 107.72 billion by 2030 (The Creative Brief Africa, 2024), indicating rapid growth across both developed and developing markets.

Beyond simple endorsement, influencer marketing increasingly operates as a multi-actor system involving platforms, brands, agencies, creators, and audiences. Ye et al. (2024) describe the creator economy as a value chain in which content creation, distribution, analytics, and monetization mechanisms jointly shape market outcomes. This perspective is useful for comparing Ghana and Côte d’Ivoire because it shifts attention from individual influencers to the supporting infrastructure that enables consistent collaboration—such as talent management, pricing norms, campaign briefing, and measurement practices. Where these components are weak or absent, the market tends to rely on informal deals that are difficult to scale and hard to evaluate.

In developing markets, influencer marketing also competes with long-established promotional channels. Brands may still prioritize radio, television, billboards, and event sponsorship because they are familiar, legally clearer, and perceived as more controllable. The literature therefore suggests that influencer marketing adoption is not only a function of social media usage; it depends on managerial capabilities, trust, and the availability of tools that reduce uncertainty around performance and accountability.

2. Influencer Credibility and Consumer Behavior

Coutinho, Dias, and Pereira (2023) examined influencer credibility—which includes expertise, trustworthiness, and attractiveness—and its significant positive impact on brand equity and consumer purchase intention. Their analysis confirmed that all three credibility aspects contribute to positive brand equity outcomes. This framework applies directly to Côte d’Ivoire, where the professional credibility of influencers is less developed compared to their Ghanaian peers. Ohanian (1990) similarly found that source credibility is the main way through which endorsers affect audience attitudes and purchasing behavior, a model still important in influencer marketing research.

Empirical evidence also highlights that credibility is context-dependent. Suganya et al. (2024) emphasize that audiences evaluate influencer claims through cues such as content quality, consistency, transparency, and perceived expertise in a niche. In practice, this means that a creator who is highly influential in lifestyle entertainment may not be equally persuasive for products requiring technical knowledge or high perceived risk (for example, financial services, health-related products, or education services). Where influencer markets are less professionalized, inconsistent posting, unclear brand fit, and weak disclosure practices can reduce trustworthiness, limiting conversion even when follower counts are high.

The credibility framework is particularly relevant for Côte d’Ivoire because audiences may associate influencer activity with entertainment and social drama rather than informed recommendations. When the informational value of content is low, Lou and Yuan’s (2019) model suggests that purchase intention is less likely to follow from exposure. Therefore, improving credibility may require not only influencer behavior (e.g., clearer niche positioning and stronger content standards) but also brand-side changes such as better briefing, product education, and realistic campaign objectives.

3. Parasocial Relationships and Brand Persuasion

Liu and Zheng (2024) applied the Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM) of persuasion to the context of influencer marketing. They discovered that the informativeness, authenticity, and similarity of influencer content positively affect parasocial relationships (PSRs) between influencers and their followers, which in turn enhances purchase intention and perceived brand credibility. Their study also confirmed that followers' persuasion knowledge can negatively affect the link between PSR and brand credibility—a finding relevant to Côte d'Ivoire, where audiences may be less aware of paid content. Horton and Wohl (1956) laid the groundwork for much of this research with their theory on parasocial interactions, explaining how media audiences create one-sided emotional connections with media personalities.

The ELM lens is important because it explains why some influencer campaigns succeed even when audiences are exposed to a large volume of advertising. When followers process messages through the peripheral route, cues such as attractiveness, humor, or familiarity can drive positive attitudes quickly. However, for higher-involvement decisions, audiences rely more on central processing, which increases the importance of evidence, product demonstrations, and credible explanations. In emerging influencer ecosystems, campaigns often overemphasize entertainment while underinvesting in informational content, reducing the likelihood that followers will move from attention to purchase.

Parasocial relationships can also be fragile in contexts where audiences suspect manipulation or feel that influencers promote anything for money. This links directly to disclosure debates. If sponsored content is not disclosed or is disclosed inconsistently, followers may initially respond positively but later develop skepticism once they recognize persuasive intent. Liu and Zheng (2024) indicate that persuasion knowledge can weaken the effect of PSR on brand credibility, which suggests that long-term effectiveness may depend on maintaining transparent, value-adding communication rather than relying on hidden advertising.

4. Ghana's Influencer Marketing Ecosystem

Ghana has become a leading example of organic creator economy development in a lower-middle-income setting. The Meridian Brief (2024) noted that Ghana's 70% internet penetration and young population create favorable conditions for influencer marketing. Ghanaian micro-influencers achieve Instagram engagement rates of 5–8%, well above the global average of 2–3%, making them attractive partners for businesses (MicroCreatorr, 2025). Gisela Amponsah (@giselaamponsah), with over 373,000 TikTok followers and 19.2 million likes, revealed that around 90% of her content consists of paid brand partnerships presented in a way that audiences do not see the commercial nature (Nkonkonsa, 2025). She works with brands such as Vaseline, Coca-Cola, and Fashion Nova. Princess Ama Burland (@princess_burland) was awarded Lifestyle, Instagram, and YouTube Influencer of the Year at the 2023 Pulse Influencer Awards and partners with companies like Coca-Cola, Nivea, Dove Ghana, and Bolt (Ameyaw Debrah, 2024). Additionally, @dulcies.life is among a growing group of Ghanaian lifestyle creators with strong brand partnership portfolios. This ecosystem is supported by structured enablers, including Portals Pitch events, creator agencies, and platforms like Wowzi, which allowed Dentsu to engage 7,432 influencers across 18 brands in six months (Wowzi, 2024).

These examples illustrate how ecosystem elements reduce transaction costs for brands. When agencies and platforms provide standardized onboarding, campaign briefs, rate guidance, and reporting dashboards, collaboration becomes easier to plan and evaluate. From the creator perspective, professional networks and events can improve skills in content strategy, negotiation, and compliance, which supports consistent brand-safe delivery. For comparative purposes, Ghana can be treated as a case where the “market-making” components of the creator economy (Ye et al., 2024) are more visible, enabling routine collaborations and clearer expectations between brands and influencers.

5. Côte d'Ivoire's Social Media and Influencer Landscape

Côte d'Ivoire's influencer ecosystem features notable figures such as Emma Lohoues (@emmalohoues, 4 million Instagram followers), Maa Bio (@maabio), @officialnad__, @zeinababbey, and @pstbadjess. Despite their large followings, these influencers often lack the brand collaboration infrastructure found in Ghana. A key cultural aspect is the dominant use of social media for entertainment, gossip, and social buzz rather than commercial exploration. This aligns with what Lou and Yuan (2019) identify as the difference between entertainment value and informational content value; entertainment drives virality, while informational content is more reliably linked to purchase intentions. StarNgage's (2024) assessment of Côte d'Ivoire identified a digital divide between urban and rural areas, payment infrastructure issues complicating influencer compensation, analytical shortcomings, and unclear advertising disclosure rules as additional obstacles.

In addition to infrastructure constraints, the Ivorian context raises questions about the “fit” between audience expectations and commercial messaging. If social media is primarily treated as a space for humor, trends, and social commentary, followers may resist overt persuasion or interpret brand partnerships as inauthentic. This can create a cycle in which brands see weak results, reduce investment, and thereby limit the opportunities for creators to develop more professional practices. Therefore, Côte d’Ivoire’s challenge is not only to increase collaboration volume but also to improve the strategic design of collaborations—selecting appropriate influencer niches, building campaigns around audience needs, and using formats that combine entertainment with credible information.

6. Barriers to Influencer Marketing in Developing Markets

The academic literature identifies several common barriers to influencer marketing in developing markets. Monetization infrastructure limitations pose a challenge. Global platforms like TikTok and YouTube restrict creator funding programs in many Francophone West African markets, forcing creators to depend solely on brand deals, which are already limited by brands' reluctance to invest. Moreover, a lack of trust between brands and influencers is another significant barrier. Many African brands prefer traditional advertising due to difficulties in measuring ROI and concerns about credibility. Weismueller et al. (2020) showed that advertising disclosure and source credibility are crucial for campaign effectiveness, highlighting that markets without professional disclosure norms create audience skepticism and brand hesitance.

Measurement is a central barrier because it affects decision-making on both sides. When brands cannot connect influencer content to concrete indicators (clicks, leads, sign-ups, coupon redemptions, or sales), they may default to “vanity metrics” like reach and likes. This can lead to disappointment if these metrics do not translate into business outcomes. Conversely, creators may find it difficult to justify rates if they cannot provide performance evidence beyond audience size. From a market development perspective, stronger analytics practices, clearer campaign objectives, and simple attribution tools (such as trackable links and codes) can reduce uncertainty and increase willingness to invest.

Disclosure and transparency remain equally important. Weismueller et al. (2020) show that disclosure interacts with source credibility in shaping purchase intention, which implies that hiding sponsorship is not always the best strategy for long-term effectiveness. Instead, professional disclosure norms can protect audience trust and reduce brand risk, especially in environments where misinformation and “fake influence” (inflated followers or engagement) may be a concern. These challenges align with practitioner discussions in African markets, where brands seek credible creators and reliable reporting, while creators seek fair contracts and predictable payment terms.

Finally, the broader business environment matters. In contexts where online payment adoption is uneven and logistics are inconsistent, consumer purchase behavior may not respond quickly to social media persuasion even when attitudes improve. This can make influencer marketing appear ineffective when the real constraint lies further down the funnel. The comparative approach in this study is therefore useful because it can distinguish between barriers that are primarily ecosystem-related (agencies, platforms, measurement, disclosure) and barriers that are market-related (payments, distribution, and consumer readiness).

VI. Methodology (Selected Elements)

3. Sample Size and Sampling Technique

For this study, we will select a group of respondents from Côte d'Ivoire and another from Ghana. Each national sample will target about 60 influencers or content creators and 40 brand or marketing professionals. In the qualitative phase, we will conduct 20 in-depth interviews, 10 per country, consisting of 6 creators and 4 brand or agency professionals each. We will use a purposive sampling technique for the qualitative interviews to ensure participants have direct experience with influencer marketing. For the quantitative survey, we will use snowball sampling, which is suitable due to the connected nature of the influencer community. This will be combined with convenience sampling to reach brand professionals through LinkedIn and industry associations.

4. Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

The inclusion criteria include individuals who are active on at least one social media platform with a minimum of 1,000 followers and at least six months of content creation experience. It also includes brand professionals directly responsible for digital marketing decisions. Participants must be based in Abidjan or Accra at the time of the study and be 18 years or older. They must also be willing to provide informed consent. The exclusion criteria include influencers whose accounts are private, inactive for more than six months, or entirely non-commercial. It also includes brand professionals who work only in traditional media roles and participants who cannot communicate in English or French, as the study tools will be administered in both languages.

5. Data Collection Methods

We will collect primary data using structured online questionnaires distributed through Snapchat, WhatsApp, and Instagram Direct Message. The questionnaire will include closed-ended and scale items adapted from validated influencer marketing scales. These will cover perceived barriers to brand collaboration, motivations for social media use, brand trust in influencer marketing, and experiences with content monetization. The instrument will be available in both English and French. We will collect qualitative data through semi-structured in-depth interviews conducted via Zoom or WhatsApp video calls. Each interview will last 20 to 30 minutes, using separate guides for creators and brand professionals.

6. Data Analysis Techniques

We will analyze the quantitative data using descriptive statistical methods, including percentages, frequency tables, and charts. This will summarize respondents' experiences and identify patterns in perceived barriers across the two countries. We will use independent samples t-tests or Mann-Whitney U tests to find significant cross-national differences. SPSS will be used for quantitative analysis. We will integrate mixed methods at the interpretation stage, putting the quantitative findings in context with qualitative insights.

7. Ethical Considerations

We will follow ethical principles throughout the research process. All participants will receive a detailed information sheet explaining the study's purpose, procedures, data usage, and their rights. We will obtain written informed consent from all participants, or we will seek verbal confirmation of consent to record at the start of each interview. We will maintain anonymity and confidentiality throughout. Survey responses will be anonymous, and interview participants will be given pseudonyms in all research outputs. Participation will be entirely voluntary, and participants may withdraw at any time without facing any consequences.
